Immediate actions

The first 48 hours

Before anything else — handle these five items. They separate a smooth grow from a preventable disaster.

01 · Critical

Light acclimation

Single STIXX 50 at 20% power, 40–45 cm above canopy. The center directly under the bar sees 400–500+ PPFD at 30 cm — far too much for a transitioning clone. Start gentle: ~100–150 PPFD.

02 · Critical

Open every vent

Without the CAN RK125 installed, CO₂ depletes and humidity climbs within hours. Open all lower intake flaps and upper exhaust ports. Keep pantry door fully open. Monkey Fan blowing horizontally across canopy toward an open upper vent — leaves should visibly rustle.

03 · Important

Yellow sticky traps

One at canopy height, one near soil surface. Clones are the #1 vector for spider mites and thrips entering an indoor grow. Check daily.

04 · Important

Inspect the clone

30× loupe or phone magnifier. Examine undersides of every leaf: tiny dots (mites), silver streaks (thrips), fine webbing, sticky residue (aphids). If anything suspicious → neem spray before entering the tent.

05 · Equipment

pH pen + min/max hygrometer

Basic pH pen (€12–15) and digital hygrometer with memory (Govee Bluetooth, €10–15). You need to know water pH before every watering and what temp/RH does during the 6-hour dark period.

Light intensity ramp over 14 days — never adjust dimmer AND height simultaneously
Days 1–7

Week 1 — Stabilize & Clone

Days 1–3 · Gentle introduction
Light at 20%, water only if dry
STIXX 50 at 20% power, 40–45 cm. 18/6 schedule (on ~7:45 AM, off ~1:45 AM). Check soil moisture by inserting a finger 2–3 cm deep — in a 7L plastic pot, the soil stays moist 3–4 days between waterings. When dry, water with 400–500 ml dechlorinated, room-temp water (let tap sit 24h to off-gas chlorine — chlorine kills mycorrhizae). Pour slowly in 2–3 passes. Do not water to runoff — you flush beneficial microbes.

Target water pH: 6.3–6.8. Linz tap runs 7.0–7.5 — a few drops pH-down brings it in range. Zero nutrients. Your tomato earth has 4–6 weeks of nutrition pre-loaded.
Day 3 · First light increase
Raise dimmer to 40%
If leaves are flat or gently "praying" upward, vibrant green, no curling → increase to 40% / 35–40 cm (~200–300 PPFD). Adjust only one variable at a time. On Day 4, add 5 ml unsulphured blackstrap molasses per liter to the next watering — feeds mycorrhizae and soil bacteria. Repeat weekly.
Days 4–5 · Most critical task
Take 2–3 backup clones
Cut 2–3 lower side branches (not the main topped growth tips). Each 10–15 cm with 2–3 nodes. Sterilize blade with alcohol, cut at 45° below a node, trim lower leaves, halve remaining fan leaves, dip 2 cm into Clonex gel (€12 at any Linzer Growshop), insert into moist Rapid Rooter or rockwool. Dome with clear plastic. 22–25°C, 80–90% RH, indirect light only. Mist 1–2×/day. Roots in 7–14 days.

This costs under €20 and is the only way to protect genetics you cannot replace.
Days 6–7 · Second light arrives
Install 2nd STIXX 50
Mount both bars parallel, 25–30 cm apart, centered. Start the new bar at 20% while original stays at current setting. Acclimate 2–3 days before increasing both. Together at 60%, the dual bars deliver ~350–450 PPFD — excellent for active veg.
Cross-section: passive ventilation with clip fan chimney effect. Leaves should visibly rustle.
Day 7–10

The transplant — 7L plastic → 11L fabric

Transplant when roots are visible at drainage holes, pot dries within 2 days, and the plant has 4–5+ nodes. Complete before starting LST — you'll anchor training ties to the new pot.

Mycorrhizae go directly on the root ball surface and in the hole — not bulk-mixed into soil
Days 8–14

Week 2 — Recover, Train, Ramp

Days 8–10 · Post-transplant recovery
Hold light at 20–40%, monitor
Both bars at 20–40%, 35–40 cm (~200–300 PPFD). Fabric pots dry faster than plastic but the new soil is uncolonized — expect first watering 3–4 days after transplant, then gradually every 2–3 days. Volume: 500–800 ml. Continue plain water + weekly molasses. No nutrients for another 3–4 weeks.
Day 10 · CalMag watch begins
Monitor for deficiency signs
Under LED, Birthday Cake lineage has higher-than-average CalMag demand. Watch for: brown spots on newer leaves (calcium), interveinal yellowing on older leaves (magnesium). Linz tap water is typically hard (>150 ppm TDS) — you likely have enough naturally. If using filtered/soft water, be ready to add 1–1.5 ml CalMag/L starting Week 3–4.
Days 10–12 · The fun begins
Begin LST training
Start only after visible recovery — new growth, leaves praying, no droop. Water first (hydrated stems bend, dry stems snap). Anchor main stem at base with soft tie to fabric pot rim. Bend two topped branches outward into a wide V-shape, secured to pot edge with rubber-coated garden wire (€3–5, any Baumarkt). Target 45° initially, flatten over days. As lower branches respond by growing up → tie those down too.

Complete all training during veg — the Birthday Cake lineage herms under stress near flip.
Days 12–14 · Full veg intensity
Ramp both bars to 80%+
Target: both STIXX 50 at 80–100%, 30–35 cm above canopy (~450–600 PPFD). Monitor for "taco-ing" (leaves curling upward lengthwise = light/heat stress). If seen → back off one dimmer step or raise bars 5 cm. Continue daily LST adjustments.
Top-down view: spreading topped branches into 4 quadrants for maximum light interception

Daily check

Reading your plant

Two minutes each morning. These signals tell you more than any instrument.

Healthy signs
  • Leaves angled upward ("praying")
  • Vibrant medium-green color
  • New growth visible at tips
  • Stems firm, slightly flexible
  • White roots at fabric pot edges
Trouble — act within 24h
  • Leaf tips browning = nutrient burn → plain water
  • Drooping + wet soil = overwatering → skip next water
  • Taco leaves = light/heat stress → dim or raise
  • White powder on leaves = PM → remove, improve airflow
  • Mushy stem base = damping off → emergency clone NOW

IPM protocol

Pest prevention

The Citron Givré's dense Cookies-lineage foliage creates micro-environments where pests thrive undetected.

Weekly spray

Neem oil — veg only

1–2 tsp cold-pressed neem oil + 2–3 drops liquid soap per liter warm water. Spray all leaf surfaces including undersides just before lights-off. Oil droplets act as tiny lenses under light → burns. Every 7–10 days. Stop before flowering.

Soil defense

Fungus gnat barrier

Top-dress soil with 1–2 cm perlite after transplanting — creates a dry barrier that prevents egg-laying. Let top centimeter dry between waterings. Yellow sticky traps catch adults.

Every 5–7 days

30× loupe inspection

Undersides of 5–6 leaves across different plant sections. Tiny moving dots (mites), silver streaks (thrips), sticky residue (aphid honeydew), fine webbing (spider mites advanced).

Devil's advocate

Ventilation urgency: Running a tent without active extraction is a stopgap, not a plan. One plant in early veg survives on passive airflow — but transpiration increases exponentially. Without the CAN RK125 by end of Week 2, you'll see CO₂ depletion (stunted growth), humidity spikes (mold risk), and heat pockets. The open-door strategy also leaks odor — and the Citron Givré is described as extremely pungent even in veg.

Tomato earth risk: Austrian Tomatenerde typically carries 200–450 mg/L nitrogen plus mineral salts. In a 30–40% blend with living soil, this is on the edge of burning a young clone's roots. Watch leaf tips obsessively. Mild tip burn = the soil is working. Progressing burn = flush with plain pH'd water until runoff, once. The transplant dilutes the hot soil with fresh mix — this is partially why the timing matters.

Manual light schedule: Inconsistent photoperiod is the #1 source of stress-induced hermies in home grows. A €5 mechanical Zeitschaltuhr from Hornbach eliminates this risk entirely. With an irreplaceable Birthday Cake cut that is already hermie-prone, this is not where you save money. Set two phone alarms as backup until you have the timer.

The three-action rule: If you do nothing else in these 14 days — (1) take backup clones by Day 5, (2) get ventilation installed by end of Week 2, and (3) resist the urge to feed. Your soil has 4–6 weeks of nutrition. The most likely way to harm this plant is by adding nutrients on top of an already-charged medium.
