Section 01

The central equation

Why every seed is different — and why phenohunting is both necessary and rewarding.

Every phenohunt begins with a single insight: two seeds from the same cross can produce radically different plants. One may grow short and dense with fuel-soaked trichomes; its sibling may stretch tall with fruity terpenes and wispy buds. The reason is encoded in a deceptively simple equation.

Phenotype = Genotype × Environment

The genotype is the plant's complete genetic blueprint — the unique combination of alleles inherited from mother and father across cannabis's 20 chromosomes and approximately 38,000–40,000 protein-coding genes. The phenotype is everything you can actually see, smell, smoke, and measure. And the environment — light, temperature, humidity, nutrients, soil biology — acts as a modifier that determines which genetic potential gets expressed and to what degree.

This interaction is profound. Even genetically identical clones grown in different environments develop measurably different phenotypes. Dutch Passion's Blueberry, for example, produces green buds at warm temperatures but vivid purple coloration when nighttime temperatures drop below 18°C — same genotype, dramatically different phenotype. This is why controlling environmental variables during a pheno hunt is not optional but essential: inconsistent conditions make it impossible to distinguish true genetic variation from microclimate noise.

Genotype × Environment = Phenotype
The fundamental equation of phenohunting. Same seed pack, same parents — but different allele combinations and environmental interactions produce visibly distinct plants.

Cannabis is one of the most genetically diverse cultivated plants on Earth. The 2025 cannabis pangenome found up to 20× more genetic variation within the species than exists among all humans. This extraordinary diversity is what makes phenohunting both necessary and rewarding: a single pack of seeds contains more phenotypic potential than most growers realize. Every elite clone-only cultivar in existence — GG4, Girl Scout Cookies, Chemdog, Zkittlez — began as a single remarkable plant discovered among many siblings through this process.

Section 02

Genetics primer — alleles, dominance & generations

The molecular foundation of why siblings differ — and which generation gives you the best selection opportunity.

Homozygous vs. heterozygous — why cannabis is variable

Every trait in cannabis is governed by one or more alleles — variant forms of a gene. A plant that carries two identical alleles for a trait (e.g., PP) is homozygous and breeds true for it. A plant carrying one of each (Pp) is heterozygous — it expresses the dominant allele's trait but silently carries the recessive one.

Cannabis, as a naturally dioecious obligate outcrosser, tends strongly toward heterozygosity. Breeder Sam the Skunkman has stated: all cannabis is heterogeneous, and that won't change soon. This inherent heterozygosity is precisely why phenohunting is necessary — and why siblings from the same cross can look radically different.

Mendelian Inheritance — Single Trait (e.g., Purple vs. Green)
Classic Mendelian 3:1 ratio for a single-gene trait. Real cannabis traits involve many genes, creating far more complex ratios and rarer combinations.

Generations — where variation lives

Seed Generations & Phenotypic Variation
Generation types and their phenotypic variation. F2 is the phenohunter's generation — maximum variation reveals both the best and worst genetic combinations.

F1 seeds from true inbred parents are remarkably uniform due to hybrid vigor, but most commercial cannabis "F1s" aren't from true inbred lines, so they show more variation than the label implies. F2 seeds unleash Mendelian segregation — hidden recessive alleles resurface in new combinations, creating what breeders call a "phenotypic explosion." This is where you'll find both your best and worst plants. S1 (selfed) seeds concentrate a single parent's genetics but with Mendelian ratios still active for any heterozygous traits. Backcrosses progressively recover a specific parent's genetic contribution — BX3 ("cubing") reaches 93.75% of the recurrent parent's DNA.

Devil's Advocate Review

F1 marketing deception. Many seedbanks label seeds "F1" when the parent lines weren't truly inbred. A cross between two individual plants from different strains is technically an F1, but without 6+ generations of inbreeding on each parent line, the offspring won't show true F1 uniformity or hybrid vigor. Treat commercial "F1" labels with skepticism — expect F2-level variation unless the breeder documents their inbreeding history.

Feminized S1 confusion. Many seedbanks sell feminized versions of named cultivars made by selfing or sister-crossing. These are functionally S1 or F2 (depending on method) with reduced genetic diversity. They produce evaluable phenotypes but with a narrower range than regular F2s from the same cross.

Section 03

Seed types and what they mean for your hunt

Regular, feminized, and autoflower seeds each carry different implications for selection scope, space efficiency, and cloning feasibility.

Seed Type Comparison
Seed type determines your selection options. Regular seeds maximize diversity but waste space on males. Feminized seeds maximize evaluable candidates. Autoflowers are impractical for pheno hunting because cloning doesn't work.
Section 04

How many seeds to pop — the statistics

Probability math, population size, and the case for depth over breadth.

The math is straightforward for simple traits: to have a 95% probability of finding at least one plant expressing a single-gene recessive trait in an F2 population (expected frequency 25%), you need approximately 12 plants. But cannabis traits are rarely controlled by single genes, and the probability of finding an ideal combination of multiple independently segregating traits drops exponentially.

The Counter-Argument — Depth Over Breadth

Breeder Mean Gene of Aficionado/Freeborn Selections selected his legendary Cherry Lime from just five females and one male in the F1 generation. His philosophy: intimate evaluation across multiple seasons trumps statistical brute force. Both approaches — large populations with rapid elimination and small populations with deep assessment — have produced legendary genetics. The choice depends on your space, time, and evaluation skill.

Section 05

The staged elimination pipeline

Professional phenohunting follows a funnel-shaped elimination pipeline — successive rounds of a tournament, narrowing from many candidates to one confirmed keeper.

Each round eliminates the weakest and promotes the strongest. Think of it as successive rounds of a tournament. The critical logistical step — clone collection in Stage 2 — is non-negotiable: miss it and you cannot recover your keepers.

Stage 1 · Weeks 0–3
Germination & Seedling
Pop all seeds in solo cups. Cull deformities, non-germinators, and severe runts. Inspect root balls at transplant — vigorous white roots are a positive sign. Eliminate approximately 15–25% at this stage: failed germinations, deformities, and half-size runts.
Stage 2 · Weeks 3–8
Veg Screening & Clone Collection
Critical step: take 4–6 clones of every remaining candidate before the flip. Assess vigor, stem rub aroma, node spacing, and branching. Remove males if running regulars. Maintain a clone library on 18/6 while mothers go into flower. Eliminate approximately 50% of regulars (males) plus weak females.
Stage 3 · Weeks 8–18+
Flowering Evaluation
Flower originals while clones remain in veg hold. Monitor stretch, bud development, and aroma progression. Watch for hermaphrodite traits — any intersex expression means immediate elimination plus clone disposal. Eliminate hermies, low-trichome candidates, poor structure, and mold-susceptible phenotypes.
Stage 4 · Weeks 18–26
Harvest, Cure & Smoke Test
Proper dry (10–14 days) and cure (minimum 2–4 weeks) before any testing. Conduct blind sensory evaluation: effect, flavor, smoothness — with multiple independent testers. Narrow to the top 2–4 candidates at this stage.
Stage 5 · Weeks 26–40+
Clone Verification Run
Flower clones of finalists side-by-side under identical conditions. Seed plants often perform differently from their clones — this step is essential, not optional. A keeper designation without clone verification is premature.
Stage 6 · Total timeline 12–24 months
Confirmed Keeper
Consistent across 2–3 complete clone runs. From 20 seeds to one confirmed keeper. Every elite clone-only cultivar followed this path.
The #1 Logistical Failure

If you don't clone every candidate before flowering, the hunt fails. By the time you identify keepers at harvest, it's too late to take cuttings — the plant is done. Take 4–6 cuttings per candidate during late veg, label everything with matching codes, and maintain clones under 18/6 light while mothers flower. This is non-negotiable.

Section 06

Morphological markers — what to read in veg

Structure, stem diameter, node spacing, and leaf architecture carry genuine predictive information — when read with the right caveats.

The stem rub technique

From approximately 3–4 weeks of vegetative growth, pinch and rub your fingers along the stem, then smell. The terpenes released reflect the plant’s genetic profile — a plant that gives off the most aroma at this early stage will usually be the loudest by flowering. Resinous stems often correspond to potency potential. The technique works best when you already know what aroma you are hunting for — it is more useful for comparing siblings than for absolute quality assessment.

What stem thickness, node spacing, and leaf shape tell you

Vegetative Morphology Indicators
Vegetative morphology indicators and trait heritability values. High H2 means selection pressure is effective; yield at H2=0.30 means environment dominates over genetics for that trait.

Stem diameter growth rate is one of the most reliable early predictors of yield potential, with a heritability of H2 = 0.52–0.56. Node spacing predicts cola structure — tight internodes forecast dense, stacked bud formation preferred for indoor. Height has the highest heritability (H2 = 0.78), making it the most selection-responsive trait, while yield (H2 = 0.30) is harder to select for because environment dominates.

Section 07

Trichome science — separating good from great

Not all frost is equal. Understanding trichome types, density, head size, and maturity timing is the difference between visual selection and scientific selection.

Cannabis Trichome Types
Three trichome types. Only capitate-stalked trichomes meaningfully contribute to potency and flavor. Evaluate density, head size, and onset timing with a 30-60x loupe.

For selection purposes, track “first visible frost day” — early onset of heavy trichome production suggests genetic predisposition toward high resin output. The genotype effect is significant: research found “Space Queen” produced significantly more capitate trichomes than “Moby Dick” under identical conditions. Larger trichome heads contain more resin — a trait worth selecting for.

Section 08

Red flags and elimination criteria

Hermaphroditism is the most consequential disqualifier — but it is one of several elimination triggers that must be applied without hesitation.

Hermaphrodite Identification
Any plant showing pollen sacs or nanners must be eliminated along with its clones. This trait is highly heritable and will compromise an entire crop if left unchecked.
Additional Elimination Criteria

Beyond hermaphroditism, eliminate candidates showing: poor vigor (plants at half sibling size by week 3 will rarely recover), susceptibility to powdery mildew or botrytis under normal conditions, loose/airy flower structure inconsistent with the genetic background, and flat or generic aromas that do not develop complexity through flowering. Studies indicate 5–10% of cultivated plants may show intersex traits under various conditions — genetic lines known for instability include OG Kush, Chemdog derivatives, GG4, and many Cookies crosses.

Section 09

Sensory evaluation — testing with rigor

Systematic blind testing eliminates confirmation bias and the trap of anti-pot — a phenotype that grows beautifully but fails where it matters most.

The most common failure in phenotype evaluation is allowing subjective preference and confirmation bias to override systematic assessment. DJ Short’s concept of anti-pot — a phenotype that grows beautifully, smells incredible, and is completely lacking in desirable effects — haunts phenohunters who skip rigorous testing.

Blind Testing Protocol

Label samples with codes only, have a separate person prepare them. Use consistent consumption methods — vaporizers at fixed temperatures (365–410F / 185–210C) are preferred for flavor assessment because they eliminate combustion byproducts. Standardize dose at 0.1–0.25g per session. Use a minimum of 3–5 independent testers, discarding statistical outliers and looking for consensus.

Score each sample on: onset speed (minutes), peak intensity (1–10), duration, effect character (cerebral vs. body, energizing vs. sedating), flavor complexity, smoothness, aftertaste, and ash quality. Write notes at set intervals — onset, +15 min, +30 min, +60 min.

Section 10

Hash wash testing & lab analysis

Not all frosty strains make great hash. The wash test and the string test reveal resin quality that visual inspection alone cannot.

For extraction-focused phenohunting, ice water hash wash testing is indispensable. Not all visually frosty strains produce quality hash — some have trichome heads that will not separate cleanly from stalks in water. The “string test” involves collecting resin on black nitrile gloves, pressing between thumb and finger, then slowly pulling apart: long, elastic strands indicate good wash potential and melt quality.

Section 11

Clone management & preservation

The mechanics of cutting, rooting, labeling, and long-term mother plant management — including the emerging science of epigenetic drift.

Clone Preservation Pipeline
Clone preservation pipeline. Take 4-6 cuttings per candidate to account for rooting failure. Rooting speed is itself a selection criterion — but some of the most potent plants are notoriously slow to root.

Mother plant maintenance & epigenetic drift

Selected keepers are maintained as mother plants under 18/6 lighting, producing 15–50 cuttings per 8-week pruning cycle. Mothers can theoretically be maintained indefinitely, but a progressive decline is widely reported and now has scientific explanation: a 2026 study found that while genetic mutations in tissue culture were minimal, epigenetic changes accumulated progressively, affecting genes involved in cannabinoid and terpene production. This explains why clones from aging mothers sometimes underperform earlier generations. Best practice: refresh mother stock every 1–3 years.

Tissue Culture — The Gold Standard

Tissue culture (micropropagation) offers superior long-term preservation. Meristem culture — excising the 0.1–0.5mm growing tip — can eliminate Hop Latent Viroid (HLVd, estimated to infect ~90% of California crops), viruses, and other pathogens. A budget DIY setup costs approximately 280–400 EUR (pressure cooker, still air box, starter media). Cannabis is notably challenging for tissue culture, with strong cultivar-specific responses.

Minimum Documentation Protocol

Record plant ID, every score at every timepoint, photos at weeks 3 and 7 of flower and at harvest, clone rooting data, dry yield, cure observations, and a final keeper status decision with a one-sentence rationale. Section 13

Advanced concepts — IBL, progeny testing, epigenetics

The sophisticated tools that separate serious breeders from casual hunters: progeny testing, inbred line development, and the emerging science of epigenetics.

Progeny testing reveals what phenotype cannot

Progeny testing is the most sophisticated selection tool: cross a candidate with multiple partners, grow out the offspring, and evaluate them. The parent whose progeny consistently outperform is the superior breeder — regardless of their own appearance. This is particularly valuable for male selection, since males cannot be evaluated for flower quality directly but contribute approximately 50% of offspring genetics.

From polyhybrid chaos to IBL stability

IBL (Inbred Line) creation is the long game — producing a line that breeds true for target traits. The process requires 5–7+ generations of selected inbreeding from landraces, or 9–10+ generations from polyhybrids. Classic examples include Skunk #1 and Northern Lights, which underwent 9 repeated cycles before stabilization. Inbreeding depression typically becomes apparent within 3–6 generations in small populations — the mitigation is maintaining parallel lines and outcrossing between them when vigor declines.

Epigenetics — the hidden layer

Epigenetic modifications represent a new frontier. Mild, non-lethal stress can establish molecular memory via stable changes that persist for weeks to entire seasons. Parental cannabinoid exposure has been shown to induce epigenetic changes detectable in offspring. For phenohunters, the key takeaway: standardizing propagation environments reduces confounding epigenetic variation that obscures true genetic merit.

The chemotype classification

Cannabis Chemotype Classification
The five cannabis chemotypes. Chemotype is primarily controlled by alleles at the THCAS/CBDAS locus on chromosome 7. Molecular markers can predict chemotype from seedling tissue with 88-98% accuracy.
Section 14

The ten mistakes that waste months of work

Each of these errors is avoidable. Each has ended promising hunts before they reached their conclusion.

Mistake Why it matters Fix
1. Inconsistent environment Observed differences reflect position in the grow space, not genetics. You cannot distinguish genetic variation from microclimate noise. Uniform conditions. Rotate plant positions weekly. Document any inconsistencies.
2. Selecting too early Mid-grow guesses prove wrong constantly. Many unremarkable plants reveal exceptional qualities after proper curing. Wait for full cure (min 2–4 weeks). Confirm across 2–3 clone runs.
3. Running too few seeds A 10-pack with regulars yields ~5 evaluable females — barely enough to judge a strain's range, let alone find its best. 20+ seeds minimum. 50+ for serious selection.
4. Not cloning before flower Identify keepers at harvest and you cannot recover them. The entire hunt is wasted. Clone every candidate during late veg. No exceptions.
5. Ignoring males Males contribute ~50% of offspring genetics. Dismissing them discards half the breeding equation. Evaluate males for stem rub, vigor, structure, disease resistance.
6. Confirmation bias Your brain convinces you the most exciting plant is the best. Pattern recognition without data is wishful thinking. Blind testing. Numeric scoring. Multiple independent testers.
7. Skipping clone verification Seed plants often perform differently from their clones. A keeper designation without verification is premature. Run finalists as clones side-by-side for a full cycle.
8. Sloppy labeling One misplaced or faded tag ruins months of careful observation. Genetic identity becomes uncertain. Waterproof tags. Consistent naming convention. Photo documentation.
9. No documentation Relying on memory across a 6-month selection process guarantees errors. You cannot compare runs without records. Spreadsheet or app. Score at every stage. Keep photos.
10. Adjusting criteria post-hoc Moving goalposts to favor a preferred plant defeats the purpose of systematic selection. Results become unrepeatable. Define selection criteria and weightings before starting. Stick to them.
Devil's Advocate · Guide Review

Pressure-testing the methodology

Every claim in this guide reviewed for oversimplification, edge cases, and real-world applicability.

Warning — Solo-Cup Screening Oversimplified

The guide recommends culling runts by week 3, but some exceptionally potent genetics are notoriously slow starters. Chemdog, GG4, and many Cookies descendants show poor early vigor but produce world-class flowers. If working with genetics known for slow starts, extend the seedling evaluation window to week 4–5 before culling for vigor. Slow growth alone should not eliminate a candidate from a line with a known slow-starting history.

Warning — Stem Rub Predictive Value Overstated

While stem rub terpene intensity often correlates with final flower aroma, experienced breeders on Overgrow and ICMag regularly report plants with minimal stem rub that develop exceptional terpene profiles during flowering, and vice versa. Use stem rub for early comparison between siblings, but never as a sole elimination criterion. The correlation is real but far from 1:1.

Note — Small-Space Phenohunting Is Possible

The guide’s population recommendations (20–50+ seeds) may feel overwhelming for a grower with a single 80×80cm tent. A practical small-space approach: pop 10 seeds in solo cups, screen to 4–6 females during early veg, take clones of all, flower in the tent, evaluate, then run winner clones in subsequent cycles. It takes longer — 2–3 cycles vs. one — but works within real-world space constraints. You are trading statistical breadth for depth of evaluation, which Mean Gene’s approach validates.

Note — Hash Wash Testing Accessibility

Most home growers will not have the equipment or material volume for proper wash testing. The string test and visual trichome assessment provide reasonable proxies for resin quality. Reserve full wash testing for commercial-scale hunts or when specifically breeding for extraction.

Verdict

Guide is comprehensive and well-sourced. The two warnings above should be kept in mind — especially the slow-starter genetics caveat, which could cause premature elimination of valuable phenos. The small-space adaptation note is directly relevant to home grower reality.
