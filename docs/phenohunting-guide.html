Population Genetics · Plant Selection · Keeper Preservation

The Definitive Guide to Phenohunting

From population genetics to practical plant selection. Why every seed is different, how to read plants at each growth stage, what to measure and when, and how to run a staged elimination pipeline from germination to flower-tested keeper.

Section 01 · Foundational Concept

The Central Equation

Every seed in a pack is different. Understanding why — at the genetic level — is the prerequisite for systematic phenohunting rather than luck-based selection.

Phenotype = Genotype × Environment. Two seeds from the same pack carry the same potential gene pool but express different allele combinations. Think of it like siblings — same parents, different faces. The genotype sets the range of possibility; the environment determines where within that range each plant lands.

This framing reveals that phenohunting is two simultaneous hunts. You are searching for the best genotype — inherent genetic potential — while simultaneously providing the optimal environment to fully express it. A genetically superior plant in a degraded environment will be outperformed by a mediocre genotype in an excellent one. Both variables compound in every selection decision.

Section 02 · Genetics Primer

Alleles, Dominance & Generations

Cannabis traits are controlled by alleles — versions of genes inherited from each parent. How alleles segregate across generations determines which seed type and population size a meaningful hunt actually requires.

For any trait, a plant carries two alleles: one from the mother, one from the father. When both alleles are identical (homozygous), the trait expresses consistently. When they differ (heterozygous), one may dominate or the two may blend. This is the mechanism behind all the phenotype variation you observe seed to seed within a single pack.

F1 Hybrid
First Generation Cross
Relatively Uniform
Most commercial "designer strains" sold in seed packs are F1 hybrids. They show relatively low phenotype variation because every plant inherits the same parental allele combination across loci. Heterosis (hybrid vigor) also contributes to strong and consistent performance — this is the mechanism behind F1 reliability, not the masking of recessive traits. F1s are good for production; limited for deep pheno-hunting.
F2 Population
F1 × F1 Cross
Maximum Variation
The segregation generation. When two F1 plants cross, their heterozygous allele pairs recombine in every possible configuration. Recessive traits that were suppressed in F1 now emerge visibly across the population. This is where phenohunting is most productive — and most demanding. Need 50+ plants for meaningful selection across the full phenotype range.
IBL / Stabilized
Inbred Line
Minimal Variation
After 6+ generations of deliberate selection and inbreeding, heterozygosity is largely eliminated. Traits express consistently — "true breeding." Feminized Super Skunk sits here, which explains why phenotypic variation (including sativa expression) is rare even across large seed populations from this line.
Academic Correction — Why Feminized Seeds Show Less Variation

Feminized seeds show reduced phenotypic variation because they are typically produced from selfed or closely related female parents (S1 or narrow-cross), not because of the STS (silver thiosulfate) or CS (colloidal silver) treatment itself. Those chemical treatments merely induce female pollen production — enabling feminization. The narrow genetic base of the source parents is what reduces phenotypic spread. A feminized F2 from diverse parents would still show high variation; a feminized IBL shows low variation because the underlying genetics are already stabilised. The two factors are independent.

Section 03 · Seed Types

Regular, Feminized, Autoflower

Seed type is the single highest-impact decision in a pheno-hunt — it determines the genetic range available before you plant a single seed.

Devil's Advocate — The Foundational Choice

The single highest-impact pheno-hunting decision is not your selection methodology, your scoring rubric, or how many markers you track. It is buying regular seeds when rare phenotypes are the objective. Stabilised feminized lines like Super Skunk are specifically bred to minimise variation. Everything else — markers, staged elimination, scoring — is optimisation on top of that foundational choice. If you are serious about the sativa hunt, start with regular seeds. The leverage is there, not in selection sophistication.

Section 04 · Population Mathematics

How Many Seeds? The Probability Framework

Finding a specific rare phenotype is a numbers game with clear mathematics. The framework is sound — the input probability must be calibrated to the actual genetics you are working with.

The probability of encountering at least one instance of a trait from a population of N seeds follows a simple complementary probability formula. If the trait appears with probability p per seed, across N seeds:

P(at least one) = 1 − (1 − p)^N

Applying this with an illustrative example of p = 0.05 — used here as a worked example only, not as a confirmed rate for any specific cultivar or trait:

5 seeds: 23%  ·  10 seeds: 40%  ·  15 seeds: 54%  ·  25 seeds: 72%  ·  50 seeds: 92%
Critical Caveat — The Input Probability Is an Assumption

The exact probability of sativa expression in feminized Super Skunk — or any other specific phenotypic trait in any cultivar — is not a published, measured figure. It depends on the specific seed stock, breeder generation, and how the target trait is defined. The mathematical framework above is valid for any assumed probability, but label all inputs as assumptions, not empirical rates. For Super Skunk feminized, a realistic sativa-expression probability is likely in the 5–15% range based on community observation, not controlled study data.

The practical takeaway holds regardless of the exact input: running fewer than 10 seeds from any pack is closer to a lottery than a systematic selection process. For feminized IBL genetics where rare traits are the target, 15–25 seeds is the minimum meaningful population. For regular seeds with higher inherent variation, 10–15 seeds may be sufficient for common phenotypic variants.

Section 05 · Protocol

The Staged Elimination Pipeline

Phenohunting works through progressive narrowing — not simultaneous evaluation of all plants to the end. Each stage applies specific criteria and culls before the next stage begins.

Staged elimination: 15 seeds → vigor cull at week 2–3 → trait selection and cloning at week 3–5 → 3–4 keepers into ScrOG → 1 true keeper confirmed post-harvest by terpene profile, potency, and smoke quality.
Stage One · Week 0–1
Germination

Germinate the full population simultaneously under identical conditions. Variation in germination environment introduces non-genetic noise into early selection data — control for temperature, humidity, and medium uniformly. Do not select anything at this stage. Observation only.

Stage Two · Week 2–3
Vigor Screen

First culling pass. Remove plants showing significantly stunted growth, asymmetric development, or failure to establish root zone within the same timeframe as siblings. This is not about slow starters — it is about genuine developmental failure. Exception: Cookie and GSC lineages characteristically establish slowly through weeks 2–3. Do not cull slow starters from these genetics prematurely.

Stage Three · Week 3–5
Morphological Trait Selection

The primary selection window. Take a clone from every candidate before culling any plant — this is non-negotiable. Apply at least three independent markers before committing to any cull decision. Single-trait elimination introduces significant selection error. Score internode spacing, leaf blade width, petiole length, stem rub terpenes, branching symmetry, stress recovery speed, and root zone vigor simultaneously.

Stage Four · Week 5–6
Keeper Transplant and ScrOG

Move 3–4 selected candidates into final containers and begin ScrOG or LST training. At this point you are cultivating the keeper population with equal care — further differentiation happens during early flower. Do not exceed 4 plants for an 80×80cm tent: more plants means less canopy space per plant and degraded growing environment for each.

Stage Five · Post-Harvest
Flower Test — True Keeper Identification

The true keeper cannot be confirmed until after harvest and a proper cure. Terpene profile at its fullest expression, potency, bud structure, and the smoke quality all contribute. Keep clones of all keeper candidates alive until you have smoked cured flower from each. Analytical testing (HPLC for cannabinoids, GC-MS for terpenes) is the gold standard for objective keeper determination — subjective assessment has significant reproducibility limitations.

Section 06 · Veg-Stage Reading

Morphological Markers

What veteran growers observe between weeks 2 and 5. Each marker is one data point — selection requires convergence across multiple indicators before any plant is culled.

Morphological markers in vegetative growth allow early prediction of flowering characteristics without waiting 60–80+ days. No single marker is definitive. Three or more markers pointing in the same direction form a reliable signal. The markers below are listed in approximate order of reliability for indica/sativa differentiation.

Marker 01
Internode Spacing
Distance between nodes along the main stem. Wide spacing signals sativa expression; tight internodes signal indica. The most reliable single differentiation marker. Measure from node 3 onward — early internodes are not representative of the plant's pattern.
Marker 02
Petiole Length
Long petioles signal more sativa expression. The distance between the main stem and the base of the fan leaf blade. One of the most consistent early-veg morphological markers for sativa expression — often more reliable than leaf blade shape, which can be altered by environmental conditions.
Marker 03
Leaf Blade Width
Narrow leaflet blades = sativa expression. Broad, wide blades = indica. Assess the index leaflet of fully mature fan leaves. Compare relative width across siblings within the population — absolute width is less meaningful than the distribution within your specific seed lot.
Marker 04
Stem Rub Terpenes
Rub the stem between thumb and forefinger and assess aroma immediately. Complex, strong stem rub predicts aromatic flower. BCP expression (peppery, clove-like) is detectable through veg in most genetics. Cookie and Chem lineage caveat applies — see callout below.
Marker 05
Branching Symmetry
Well-branched, symmetrical plants with strong secondary node development produce superior ScrOG canopies and more uniform yields. Asymmetric or single-dominant-branch phenotypes are harder to train and often indicate underlying developmental stress or expression.
Marker 06
Stress Recovery Speed
How quickly a plant bounces back from topping, LST, or transplant. Plants that recover quickly handle flower-stage defoliation and ScrOG manipulation better. Slow recovery signals stress-sensitive genetics that will punish training mistakes throughout the grow.
Marker 07
Root Zone Vigor
White root tips at the edges of a fabric pot indicate aggressive healthy root development. Brown or absent root tips indicate root zone dysfunction that will limit flower production regardless of canopy quality above ground.
Marker 08
Relative Growth Rate
Sativa-expression phenos typically grow faster in early veg relative to their siblings. Compare within the population, not against breeder estimates — relative rate within your specific seed lot is informative; absolute rate is not.
Critical Caveat — Cookie and Chem Lineages: Stem Rub

For cultivars carrying Cookie (GSC) or Chemdog lineage, stem rub aroma through veg week 6–7 can be muted or completely underdeveloped. This is characteristic of these genetics and is not predictive of flower aroma. Do not use early stem rub as a negative selection marker for Cookie and Chem-lineage plants. These lines often produce their most complex terpene expression only in late flower, and early culling based on muted stem rub discards potentially exceptional phenotypes.

Academic Note — Stem Rub Limitations

Stem rub provides a preview of constitutive terpene production — terpenes expressed continuously in vegetative tissue. However, some terpene synthase genes are flower-specific in their expression patterns, meaning certain terpene compounds will not be detectable through stem rub at any vegetative stage. Stem rub is a useful positive indicator; it cannot fully characterise the eventual flower profile. GC-MS analysis of cured flower remains the only complete terpene assessment method.

Section 07 · Flower Assessment

Trichome Science — Separating Good from Great

During flower, trichome development becomes the primary selection criterion. Three types exist; only one carries the cannabinoid and terpene load that matters for selection.

Capitate-stalked trichomes with visible stalk and bulbous head contain 80%+ of cannabinoid and terpene content. Under 60× loupe: look for dense coverage of tall, fat-headed stalked trichomes on calyxes — not sugar leaves, which mature faster and produce misleading harvest-readiness signals.
Assessment Protocol — Always Read Calyxes, Not Sugar Leaves

Sugar leaf trichomes mature 5–10 days faster than calyx trichomes. Reading sugar leaves produces premature harvest decisions in virtually all cases. A 60× jeweller's loupe is adequate for field assessment of clear/cloudy/amber transition. A 100× digital microscope gives unambiguous differentiation. Always confirm harvest timing from calyx tissue only.

Section 08 · Error Prevention

Ten Mistakes That Waste Months

The most common selection errors, drawn from community post-mortems across ICMag, Rollitup, and Overgrow. Each has ended a viable pheno-hunt prematurely.

Avoid
  • Culling slow starters at week 2–3: Cookie and GSC-lineage plants are characteristically slow in early veg. Patience is itself a selection variable.
  • Judging by a single trait: Require at least three independent markers converging before any cull decision is made.
  • Selecting by bud density alone: Cannabinoid concentration is genetically controlled and shows no meaningful correlation with physical bud compactness (Potter 2012). Dense buds are not proxy for potency.
  • Forgetting to clone before culling: Once a plant is eliminated without a clone, that genetic line is gone permanently. Clone before you kill — without exception.
  • Running fewer than 10 seeds: Below 10 seeds you are running a lottery. Population size is the foundational variable before methodology matters.
Also Avoid
  • Skipping the smoke test: Terpene profile and subjective smoke quality are final selection criteria accessible only post-harvest. No veg or early flower assessment replaces them.
  • Overvaluing yield over quality: High yield is secondary. The keeper is the most enjoyable plant, not the heaviest one.
  • Selecting in a degraded environment: VPD problems, pH instability, or pest pressure mask genetic quality. You are selecting FOR the environment; a poor one produces misleading data.
  • Keeping too many keepers: Four plants is the practical maximum for an 80×80cm tent. More plants means less space per plant and a degraded environment for all.
  • Assuming feminized = identical: Feminized IBL seeds show reduced variation, not zero variation. Meaningful phenotypic diversity persists even in highly stabilised lines.
Academic Note — Bud Density and Cannabinoid Concentration

Potter (2012) established that cannabinoid concentration is genetically determined and shows no meaningful correlation with bud density or physical compactness. These are independent traits — one architectural, one biochemical — that can and do appear in every combination. Dense structure is a breeding trait with canopy and handling implications; potency is entirely separate. Do not use visual density as a proxy for cannabinoid content during phenotype selection.

Analytical Testing — The Gold Standard for Final Selection

Modern phenohunting increasingly incorporates HPLC (High Performance Liquid Chromatography) for cannabinoid quantification and GC-MS (Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry) for terpene profiling. Subjective assessment — visual inspection, stem rub, smoke evaluation — has significant limitations in precision and reproducibility between observers and sessions. When selecting a keeper for long-term propagation, analytical testing of cured flower from each candidate provides objective, comparable data. Subjective methods are useful for field screening and progressive elimination; analytical testing is the standard for final determination.

Devil's Advocate — The Meta-Mistake

The most consequential phenohunting mistake is not made during selection — it is made before germination. Running stabilised feminized seeds when the stated goal is discovering rare phenotypes guarantees a structurally constrained search. Stabilised feminized IBL lines are specifically engineered to minimise phenotypic spread. If genuine diversity is the objective, the highest leverage point is the seed choice, not the sophistication of the elimination pipeline applied to a narrow gene pool. A well-executed pheno-hunt from feminized IBL genetics will produce less variation than a casual hunt from regular F2 seed. Consider this before investing months in a selection program.
