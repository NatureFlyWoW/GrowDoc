Chapter I · Selection Framework

Terpene-First Selection Methodology

Cultivar selection driven by effect profile rather than yield metrics or breeder reputation. The core hypothesis: if you match terpene chemistry to your target experience, the genetics take care of the rest.

Most growers choose strains based on yield figures, THC percentages, or brand reputation. This library uses a different starting point: terpene profile as the primary filter, with grow characteristics and genetic stability as secondary considerations. The reasoning is straightforward — cannabinoid content is the ceiling of potency, but terpene chemistry determines the character of the experience. Two strains at 22% THC can produce radically different effects depending on their dominant terpene signatures.

The target effect profile that anchors this library is euphoric, cerebral, and uplifting with moderate body relaxation — not sedating, not racey, but clear-headed and socially functional. That profile maps to a specific terpene axis: beta-caryophyllene + limonene as primary drivers, with terpinolene or linalool adding complexity.

Research Caveat — Terpene Variability

Terpene profiles shown throughout this library are averages from aggregated analytical data. Individual plants of the same cultivar can vary significantly depending on phenotype, environment, and harvest timing (Reimann-Phillip et al. 2020). Treat all listed profiles as representative ranges, not guaranteed outputs.

Primary Target
β-Caryophyllene
Spicy, peppery, clove-like. The only terpene confirmed as a CB2 receptor agonist (Gertsch et al. 2008, PNAS). Body relaxation without sedation. Note: CB2 binding is established science. Anti-inflammatory and anxiolytic functional effects are preclinical only — not confirmed in human trials at cannabis-consumption doses.
Secondary Target
Limonene
Citrus, bright, uplifting. Associated with mood elevation and anxiolytic effects in animal models (Carvalho-Freitas & Costa 2002). Note: Anxiolytic effects have not been confirmed in controlled human trials at cannabis-relevant doses.
Tertiary
Terpinolene / Linalool
Terpinolene: fresh, piney, herbal — found in ~10% of cannabis. Associated with uplifting, almost psychedelic clarity. Linalool: floral, lavender — adds smoothness and complexity to the overall profile.
Lineage Disclaimer

All strain lineages recorded in this library are breeder-reported claims unless independently verified through genomic analysis. The cannabis seed market has historically operated without standardized genetic verification protocols, and breeder-reported ancestry should be treated as a working hypothesis rather than established fact.

Effect Disclaimer — All Strains

Effect descriptions throughout this library are based on user-reported experiences and terpene-mechanism plausibility, not controlled clinical data. Individual responses vary based on dose, tolerance, set, and setting. No effect claim in this document should be interpreted as a therapeutic or medical statement.

Stretch Correction

The "2–3× stretch" figure applies only to Haze-dominant phenos. NL5-leaning phenos express 50–80% stretch — significantly more manageable in a constrained indoor space. Identify pheno type early (leaf blade width, internode spacing) before committing to canopy management strategy. Haze phenos require aggressive training and early flip; NL5 phenos are far more tent-friendly.

Terpene Match Correction

Super Skunk is myrcene-dominant, not a BCP+Limonene strain. An honest terpene match score against that specific target is 3/5. BCP is present as a secondary terpene, contributing some body relaxation. As a beginner-friendly grow with excellent structure and robust yields, however, its forgiveness score is a genuine 5/5. These are separate qualities — do not conflate them.

Pheno Rate Correction — Sativa Expression

Sativa-leaning phenos occur at roughly 15–30% in regular seeds (not 30–50% as sometimes cited). Sensi Seeds has conducted extensive selection pressure toward indica expression over decades of production. In feminized seeds, sativa expression is considerably rarer — the feminization process introduces additional genetic selection pressure, further reducing phenotypic spread.

The β-Caryophyllene Thread

Every cultivar in the target lineup shares β-Caryophyllene as a dominant or major terpene. BCP is the only terpene confirmed as a direct CB2 receptor agonist (Gertsch et al. 2008, PNAS). CB2 binding is established. The resulting body relaxation and anti-inflammatory functional effects are supported by preclinical data but have not been confirmed in humans at cannabis-consumption doses. When pheno-hunting any strain in this library, BCP-forward stem rub terpenes — spicy, peppery, clove-like — remain a valid positive selection marker for that terpene axis.

Chapter IV · Research Notes

Devil's Advocate & Critical Analysis

Contrarian perspectives and academic caveats. The section exists because strain research frequently overstates certainty. These notes apply sceptical pressure to the most common overclaims.

Devil's Advocate — Terpene-First Selection

The terpene-first selection methodology is compelling but rests on two assumptions that remain scientifically contested. First, that the terpene profile of a commercial seed-pack will reliably match the consumer's experience — terpene data from aggregated analytical sources does not account for grow environment, phenotype variation, or harvest timing. Second, that terpene-mediated effects (especially the "entourage effect") are robustly dose-dependent at actual consumption levels. The mechanism is plausible; the dose-response relationship in humans is not well-characterised. Select by terpene profile if it aligns with your preferences, but maintain calibrated uncertainty about predictive power.

Devil's Advocate — The Lineage Confidence Problem

Cannabis strain lineages as published by breeders are marketing claims as much as botanical facts. The industry has operated without standardised genetic verification for most of its existence. Genomic analysis of well-known cultivars has repeatedly surfaced discrepancies between breeder-reported ancestry and actual genetic clustering. This is not an accusation of deliberate fraud — genetic drift, parallel development, and informal naming conventions all contribute. Treat every lineage in this library as a plausible working hypothesis, not a verified pedigree.

Academic Note — Skunk #1 Lineage

Skunk #1's sativa characteristics derive from Colombian and Mexican landrace heritage, not Durban Poison. Durban Poison is a separate South African landrace not present in the Skunk #1 lineage. This is a persistent and common error in community cultivation resources. The confusion likely originates from the co-occurrence of Durban Poison in other 1970s–80s California breeding projects that ran contemporaneously with Skunk development.

Academic Note — β-Caryophyllene Claims

CB2 receptor binding by β-caryophyllene is established science (Gertsch et al. 2008, PNAS). This library does not dispute that. However, several widely circulated cultivation resources extrapolate from this binding confirmation to claims of "confirmed anti-inflammatory" or "clinically anxiolytic" effects at cannabis-consumption doses in humans. Those functional effects are currently supported by preclinical (primarily animal model) data only. The gap between receptor binding and meaningful clinical outcome in intact humans, at inhaled or orally-consumed terpene concentrations, remains a subject of active investigation. Claims in this library are accordingly bounded.

Academic Note — Limonene Claims

Limonene anxiolytic effects have been demonstrated in animal models (Carvalho-Freitas & Costa 2002) and some human inhalation studies with isolated limonene at concentrations higher than typically achieved through cannabis consumption. Effects at cannabis-relevant doses in mixed-terpene matrices have not been established through controlled human trials. The "mood elevation" attribution in this library reflects user-reported experience and mechanistic plausibility, not confirmed clinical efficacy.

Practical Note — On Forgiveness vs. Terpene Match

The selection matrix in Chapter III separates terpene match from grower forgiveness deliberately. These are orthogonal qualities. Super Skunk scores 3/5 on terpene match against BCP+Limonene and 5/5 on forgiveness. A grower optimising for first-harvest success should weight forgiveness heavily. A grower with one successful harvest behind them can afford to prioritise terpene match. Do not let a high forgiveness score imply terpene excellence, nor a perfect terpene match imply ease of cultivation.
