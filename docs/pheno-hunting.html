Pheno-hunting sativa Super Skunk

A visual guide to germinating 10–15 feminized Super Skunk seeds in your Q80+ tent, identifying sativa-leaning phenotypes as early as week 2–3, selecting the best 4 keepers, and preserving genetics via clones — all within the constraints of an 80×80cm tent under a single Caluma Force 150W LED. Devil's advocate reviewed throughout.

Devil's advocate — honest odds: Feminized Super Skunk is heavily stabilized for indica uniformity. Sensi Seeds markets the feminized line as producing "an extremely uniform crop." From 15 feminized seeds, expect 0–1 plants with genuinely sativa-leaning traits, versus 4–7 from regular seeds. The pheno-hunt process is still valuable — it selects the 4 strongest performers regardless — but if finding the sativa pheno is your primary objective, regular seeds would dramatically improve your odds. This guide assumes you're working with feminized seeds and want to maximise your chances within that constraint.

1. What you're looking for — the four Super Skunk phenotypes

SeedFinder's database of 26 indoor grow reviews, cross-referenced with ICMag breeder reports, identifies four distinct phenotypes along two axes: plant structure (tall vs. short) and flowering speed (fast vs. slow). Most feminized seeds express Pheno 3 (short/fast indica). The sativa treasure is Pheno 1 or 2.

The four Super Skunk phenotypes. Your target (Pheno 1, blue) is rare in feminized stock — most seeds express Pheno 3 (green, indica/fast).

2. When can you tell? — identification timeline

The earliest reliable identification window opens at nodes 3–5 (weeks 2–3 from sprout). Before that, seedlings show no meaningful indica/sativa differentiation. Here's what to watch for at each stage, in order of reliability.

Phenotype comparison chart: indica (typical, left) vs. sativa (rare target, right). Compare these traits across all 15 seedlings at nodes 3–5.

No single trait is diagnostic. Look for convergence — a plant showing narrow leaves AND wide internodes AND lighter color AND citrus stem rub is a far stronger sativa candidate than one showing any single trait alone. With 15 sister plants under identical conditions, the outliers become obvious.

3. The screening setup — fitting 15 seedlings into your Q80+

Screening phase: 15 numbered 1L pots in a 4×4 grid under the Caluma at 25–50% power. Select at week 3–4, transplant top 4 to 11L.

Devil's advocate — rootbound risk: The screening schedule is driven by root development, not phenotype readiness. In 1L pots with Light-Mix, roots begin circling aggressively at week 3–4. In 0.5L cups, this happens a week earlier. Use 1L pots to buy the critical extra observation week. If you need more time, transplant the top 6–8 into 3L pots temporarily, then select the final 4 for 11L — but this adds a week of veg time and an extra transplant stress event.

5. Clone backup — preserving genetics in a tiny space

When you top the best 6–8 candidates at day 21–28, each removed shoot tip becomes a clone cutting. This is the single most valuable strategy for a small-space pheno-hunt — it lets you preserve the genetics of every promising plant while only growing 4 to maturity.

Clone backup strategies for a single-tent setup. Shelf clones need a 15–20W bulb; fridge cuttings need only a cup of water.

Devil's advocate — height management: The sativa pheno's 2–3× stretch is the single biggest practical challenge in your 180cm tent. With 105cm of usable plant height (after LED, clearance, pots), a sativa pheno flipped at 35cm could reach 70–105cm — right at your ceiling. If you identify a sativa-leaning plant during screening, flip it earlier (at 25–30cm) or train it more aggressively with LST/supercropping to control height. The ScrOG net becomes absolutely essential for sativa phenos — it caps vertical growth and forces horizontal spread.
Flowering time impact: A sativa pheno taking 9–10 weeks to flower adds 2–3 weeks to your overall timeline compared to the indica sisters that finish in 7–8 weeks. If you find a sativa-leaning plant, plan to stagger harvest — the indica phenos will be ready first. Cut them, then give the sativa another 2–3 weeks under light. This actually works well, because the extra light (no shading from harvested neighbours) helps the sativa fatten its colas in the final stretch.

7. Decision point — feminized vs. regular seeds

This is the honest assessment you need to make before committing 3–4 weeks of tent time to a pheno-hunt.

The fundamental trade-off: feminized seeds are simpler but nearly eliminate sativa expression. Regular seeds preserve full genetic diversity.
Devil's advocate — the strongest recommendation: If finding a sativa-leaning Super Skunk is genuinely your primary objective (not just a nice-to-have), acquiring regular seeds is the single most impactful decision you can make. Running regular seeds through this same protocol would realistically yield 2–4 sativa-leaning females from 15 seeds — enough to fill the tent with exactly the phenotype you're hunting. With feminized seeds, the process still produces four excellent indica-dominant plants, but the specific sativa treasure hunt has long odds against it. The pheno-hunting skills (observation, cloning, selection) transfer directly regardless of seed type.

Summary: Germinate 10–15 seeds into 1L square pots → screen for 3–4 weeks under Caluma at 30–50% → compare all 6 trait markers across sister plants → clone best 6–8 → select top 4 for 11L transplant → resume the 200g grow guide from week 5. The earliest reliable identification of sativa traits occurs at nodes 3–5 (weeks 2–3). The strongest sativa signals are narrow leaf blades + wide internodes + lighter colour + citrus stem rub converging in the same plant. Even with feminized seeds, this process selects the 4 best performers from your seed stock — a significant upgrade over random planting.
