1. Master floor plan — top-down view

This is the definitive layout. The tent is pushed right and back, creating a 40cm service corridor on the left for duct access, and leaving ~10cm clearance at the front for the door to clear the tent when opened.

Illustration 1: Master floor plan — tent pushed right against the right wall, 40cm service corridor on left, exhaust duct runs along left wall to door.
Devil's advocate — positioning: The previous diagrams centered the tent in the pantry, leaving 20cm on each side. This wastes space — neither 20cm gap is wide enough to comfortably work in. The corrected layout pushes the tent against the right wall, consolidating all clearance into a single 40cm service corridor on the left. This gives enough room to reach the duct port, adjust the fan speed, and access the power strip without contorting around the tent. The right wall still allows the OmniFlow passive intake vent to function (mesh doesn't need clearance).

2. Side elevation — vertical layout

This cross-section shows how every component stacks vertically inside the 180cm tent, and the 70cm of headroom above the tent to the 250cm ceiling. The key constraint is the 105cm of usable plant height after subtracting pot, net, LED, and clearance.

Illustration 2: Side cross-section — filter + fan hang from crossbar at top, LED centered, 105cm usable plant zone, exhaust exits through left port and routes down wall to door.
Devil's advocate — height budget: The previous guide showed the fan outside the tent at floor level. This wastes the 40cm corridor space. The corrected layout hangs the fan from the crossbar next to the filter — putting both heavy items at the top where heat naturally rises (hot air reaches the filter first). The exhaust duct then runs down the tent wall, exits through the side port, and continues along the pantry's left wall to floor level and out the door. This is longer duct (more resistance), but the fan is oversized for this tent, so the extra resistance actually helps — it naturally slows the airflow without needing the Varispeed turned as low.

3. Ventilation routing — the complete airpath

This exploded view shows every connection in the ventilation chain. The three most common smell-leak points are marked — each needs a hose clamp AND aluminium foil tape.

Illustration 3: Complete airpath with the three critical smell-leak points marked. Each connection requires a hose clamp for mechanical grip plus aluminium foil HVAC tape as the airtight seal.
Danger — the #1 stealth failure: A single untaped duct joint leaks raw, unfiltered odor into the corridor. Duct tape degrades in humid conditions within weeks — always use aluminium foil HVAC tape as the sealing layer, with a Schlauchschelle providing the mechanical grip underneath.

4. Electrical wiring — what plugs into what

The wiring chain runs from the hallway outlet through a ground-fault adapter and wall-mounted power strip, then splits to the LED (on timer), fan (on Varispeed, running 24/7), and clip fan. Total load is well within the 16A circuit capacity.

Illustration 4: Electrical wiring chain — only the LED goes on the timer. Fan runs 24/7 through the Varispeed speed controller. PRCD-S provides ground-fault protection.
Critical safety rule — never connect the exhaust fan to the timer: If the fan stops while the LED runs, tent temperature spikes within minutes in a sealed pantry. The fan provides cooling AND odor control — it must run continuously. The PRCD-S adapter (€30–50 from OBI/Hornbach) provides ground-fault protection even if your flat's breaker panel doesn't have a modern FI-Schutzschalter.

5. Door airflow — how the room breathes

Your pantry's only air exchange is the door gap. This cross-section shows the thermal stratification that makes the system work: warm exhaust exits low, cool fresh air enters high.

Illustration 5: Door cross-section showing thermal stratification — warm exhaust exits low through duct at floor level, cool fresh air enters high passively through the gap driven by negative pressure.
Devil's advocate — door gap physics: The previous guide said "prop the door open 15cm." That's more than necessary and creates a large light leak during flower. A 10cm gap provides ~2,000cm² of free area — far exceeding the ~370cm² the fan needs for passive intake. During 12/12 flower, hang blackout fabric (Verdunkelungsstoff) over the gap to prevent light reaching the tent through the gap-to-pantry-to-tent path. The tent itself is light-tight, but hallway light entering the pantry can sneak through passive intake vents.

6. Inside-tent component map

Every piece of equipment has a specific position. This shows the view looking down into the tent from above, with hanging positions on the crossbar marked.

Illustration 6: Component positions inside tent — filter + fan on left of crossbar, LED centered, clip fan on right pole. 8.1 kg of 50 kg crossbar capacity used.
Assembly order matters: Always hang the filter and fan first — reaching the crossbar with the LED already mounted and powered is awkward and risks dropping tools onto the light. Thread the duct through the tent port before hanging the fan. The LED goes on last because it's the most fragile and the ratchet hangers make it easy to adjust height later.

7. Floor protection — triple-layer system

Protecting the rental tile floor from water damage and nutrient staining. Three layers ensure zero contact between grow runoff and the Fliesen underneath.

Illustration 7: Triple-layer floor protection — 200μm Baufolie under everything (edges taped up 5cm), rigid Garland tray inside tent, fabric pots elevated on 2cm risers. Total ~€25–40.

8. Door stealth — sealing sound, smell, and light

The pantry door frame is your stealth perimeter. Sealing it properly blocks all three vectors — sound, smell, and light — in one modification.

Illustration 8: Door frame sealing — foam weatherstrip on all three frame sides + rubber sweep at floor blocks sound (15–25 dB), smell (passive diffusion), and light simultaneously.
Devil's advocate — the most impactful €15 you'll spend: The previous guide treated door sealing as optional. It isn't. In an Austrian Mietwohnung, the hallway is shared building space. A single whiff of Super Skunk in flower — one of the most pungent strains ever bred — reaching the Stiegenhaus through your hallway could end the entire project. Foam weatherstripping on the door frame + rubber sweep at the bottom is the single highest-impact stealth measure. Do this before you even assemble the tent.

Commissioning checklist — verify during the 24h test

Negative pressure
Tent walls gently bowing inward — confirms airflow is correct
Temperature
22–28°C with LED at 100% — stays in range passively
Smell check
No smell outside pantry door — carbon filter is working
Vibration
No fan vibration audible in hallway — Varispeed tuned correctly
Duct joints
All dry — no condensation leaks at sealed junctions
Power strip
Stays cool to touch — load is within spec
Pass all six checks before germinating: The 24h commissioning test catches wiring errors, seal failures, and thermal issues before any plants are at risk. If any check fails, diagnose and re-test before proceeding. Once all six pass, you're ready to germinate.
